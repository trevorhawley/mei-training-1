Lifestyle, not medicine

Happy Cat Tips

Small, low-stakes things that make a cat's life a little better. Not medical advice — just stuff we've learned from living with cats.

Tip 01

Give them a box. Any box.

Before you recycle that delivery box, leave it on the floor for 24 hours. You'll be amazed. It's free enrichment and the cat will think you did them a favor.

Tip 02

Vertical space matters

A cat tree, a cleared shelf, or even a sturdy bookcase they're allowed on. Cats need to be up high — it's how they feel safe and survey their territory.

Tip 03

Water away from food

In the wild, a water source near a kill is contaminated water. Cats often drink more if their water bowl is in a different room than their food. A small change with a big hydration impact.

Tip 04

Let them choose the spot

If your cat keeps sleeping in the linen closet, consider: the linen closet is now theirs. Fighting it is exhausting for both of you. Make the chosen spot safe and move on.

Tip 05

Slow blinks back

When a cat slow-blinks at you, they're saying "I trust you enough to close my eyes near you." Slow-blink back. It's a small ritual that builds the relationship over time.

Tip 06

One toy at a time

Don't leave all toys out at once. Rotate them weekly. A toy that disappears and reappears is far more interesting than one that's always there. Novelty is the whole game.

A note on play

The 15-minute rule

Most cats need 15–20 minutes of active play, twice a day, to be at their calmest. Not 45 minutes once a day — that overstimulates them. Short, intense sessions that end with a meal mimic the hunt-eat-groom-sleep cycle they're built for.

A wand toy with a feather end is hard to beat. Let them catch it sometimes. A cat that never "wins" the play session gets frustrated, and frustrated cats find creative ways to show it — usually at 3 AM, usually on your chest.

End the session with food, and they'll typically groom and sleep. This is the closest you'll get to a "off switch" on a cat.

These tips come from years of living with cats, not from veterinary training. If your cat's behavior changes suddenly — sleeping much more or much less, hiding for long periods, or stopping eating — please see a vet. Behavior is often the first sign of a medical issue.
