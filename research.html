Why cats sleep in weird spaces

A layperson's tour through the actual science, with the boring parts kept and the jargon removed.

Cats sleep 12–16 hours a day. Where they choose to spend those hours is not random — it's the result of millions of years of evolution telling them, very specifically, to climb into your shoe.

Here's what the research actually says, broken into the four forces that shape a cat's sleeping choices.

Force 01

Security & enclosure

Cats are both predators and prey. In the wild, sleeping in the open is a bad idea. Enclosed spaces — boxes, drawers, bags — replicate the burrows and dens their ancestors sought.

A 2014 study from Utrecht University found that cats given access to hiding boxes recovered from stress faster and showed fewer signs of anxiety than cats without them. Enclosure isn't a quirk. It's a need.

Source: Vinke et al., 2014, Applied Animal Behaviour Science

Force 02

Thermoregulation

A cat's normal body temperature is around 38.6°C (101.5°F) — higher than ours. They lose heat quickly and seek warm surfaces to conserve energy while sleeping.

This explains the keyboard (warm laptop below), the sunbeam on the rug, and the spot directly on top of you. In summer, the same drive reverses: cool porcelain sinks become appealing.

Cats prefer ambient temperatures around 30–38°C when resting.

Force 03

Scent & bonding

Cats have scent glands on their cheeks, paws, and flanks. When they sleep on your clothes, shoes, or bag, they're mixing their scent with yours — a behavior ethologists call "scent commingling."

It's a sign of affiliation. The shoe isn't just a shoe. It's a portable, scent-rich version of you that they can nap inside.

Related: cats who sleep on their owner's bed show higher attachment-security scores.

Force 04

Vantage & territory

Cats like to sleep where they can see without being seen. High shelves, the top of the fridge, the mantel — these offer a vantage point over the household's "prey pathways" (i.e., the hallway).

Vertical territory matters as much as floor space. A cat without access to high places will often compensate by sleeping somewhere inconvenient at ground level — which, from their perspective, is the same thing.

The takeaway

There's no such thing as a weird sleeping spot.

Every "weird" choice — the box, the shoe, the vase, the keyboard — is the cat solving the same four problems at once: am I safe, am I warm, do I smell my people, can I see what's coming?

The weirdness is on us. The cat is being extremely logical.
